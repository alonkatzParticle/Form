## SECTION 1: BRAND IDENTITY & VOICE DNA

### 1.1 Brand Positioning
Particle for Men is a premium men's grooming and wellness brand. Core identity pillars:
- **"Aesthetic Science"** — The brand's proprietary framing. Not clinical pharma, not trendy beauty. Science that serves masculine aesthetics.
- **"For the Contemporary Gentleman"** — Not "guys" or "dudes." The customer is a man who has achieved things and wants his appearance to match his ambition.
- **Made in USA** — Quality, precision, trustworthiness. Used as a differentiator against generic/imported alternatives.
- **1,000,000+ customers** — The social proof anchor. Referenced constantly across scripts.
- **30-day money-back guarantee** — The risk-reversal that appears in virtually every CTA.
- **ParticleForMen.com** — The conversion destination. Always written as one word: particleformen.com

### 1.2 Voice Architecture
The Particle voice operates on a spectrum. Match voice register to the creative format:

**Register 1: Deadpan / Cynical (Pattern Interrupt ads, Animated Characters, Humor-driven)**
- Short sentences. Sentence fragments welcome.
- Self-aware about being an ad. ("Is this some kind of ad?" "Not until your shampoo fears me.")
- Pop culture references to masculinity. Grills, mortgages, taxes, gym, dating.
- Sarcasm that respects the viewer: "Your deodorant still leaves white marks on every shirt you own. Embarrassing."
- Never mean-spirited. The humor punches at the problem, not the man.

**Register 2: Direct / Authoritative (Science ads, Greenscreen, Expert-led)**
- Confident assertions backed by mechanism. "Body odor doesn't come from sweat. It comes from bacteria."
- Ingredient-to-outcome chains. "Capixyl targets hair loss. Redensyl reactivates follicles."
- Clinical vocabulary used sparingly for credibility, then immediately translated to plain English.
- Doctor/expert energy without being preachy.

**Register 3: Emotional / Intimate (Partner POV, Testimonial, Story arc)**
- First-person narration. Vulnerable opening, triumphant close.
- Specific sensory details: "His skin felt smoother," "She ran her fingers through his hair," "I caught a glimpse of him in the mirror."
- The emotional stakes are always relational: looking tired → feeling invisible → partner notices → confidence returns → intimacy reignites.

**Register 4: Native / UGC (TikTok-style, Creator-led, Caption-only)**
- Unpolished. Real. Like someone talking to their phone.
- "Look… most shampoos are just soap with a nice smell."
- Comments-style hooks: "Is there actually a shampoo that smells attractive on guys?"
- Zero brand-speak. If it sounds like marketing, rewrite it.

### 1.3 Voice Anti-Patterns — NEVER DO
- Never use "skincare routine" without immediately making it feel masculine ("ritual," "upgrade," "your daily edge")
- Never say "please try" or "we'd love for you to" — command, don't beg
- Never use emoji in scripts (only in TikTok-style comment overlays)
- Never say "anti-aging" without pairing it with a specific visible outcome
- Never reference "beauty" — use "grooming," "performance," "upgrade," "results"
- Never use generic superlatives ("amazing," "incredible," "wonderful") — be specific
- Never shame men for aging — reframe aging as something they can control
- Never use "unisex" or imply the product is for everyone — it's engineered for MEN
- Never write a script that sounds like it could be for a women's skincare brand

## SECTION 2: THE HOOK ENCYCLOPEDIA — 1,037 Hooks Systematized

Analysis of 1,037 real hooks from the production board reveals 12 distinct hook formulas. Listed by frequency of use:

### 2.1 The Provocative Question (182 instances — most used)
Forces the viewer to answer internally, creating engagement before they decide to scroll.

**Formula**: [Unexpected question that targets a specific insecurity or curiosity]

**Proven examples by product**:
- Face Cream: "Why are women scared of me now?" / "What if the signals she's reading are all wrong?" / "Are you sick of the constant reminders you're not as young as you used to be?"
- Shampoo: "Ever look in the mirror and feel like... damn, when did I start looking older than I feel?"
- Neck Cream: "What if I told you the oldest-looking part of your face isn't actually your face?"
- Shaving Gel: "Does your morning shave leave you with redness, irritation, or that familiar burning sensation?"
- Ab Firming Cream: "Is Your Belly Stealing Your Spotlight?" / "Ignoring Your Midsection?"
- Anti-Gray Serum: "Gray Hair? Reverse It!" / "Tired of seeing the signs of time every day?"
- Infinite Male: "Round, puffy face? It's not salt… it's LOW T."
- Deodorant: "Your deodorant — is it actually working, or just covering up?"
- Sunscreen: "Guys, you ever think… 'what doesn't kill me makes me stronger'? Yeah… well—the big C can kill you."

**Construction rules**:
- Must be answerable with "yes" or an uncomfortable realization
- Must reference a SPECIFIC problem, not a vague concern
- Question mark is essential — it changes the cognitive processing from passive to active
- Best when the question implies the viewer is making a mistake they don't know about

### 2.2 The Vanity Mirror (114 instances)
Forces confrontation with the viewer's own appearance. Triggers mirror-checking behavior.

**Formula**: [Statement about how they look or how others perceive their appearance]

**Proven examples**:
- "Tired of looking tired?"
- "You Look Different."
- "Not gonna lie… turning 45 hit harder than I expected."
- "Before vs. After — Look Closer!"
- "That's what someone told me last week. Again." (after "You look tired")
- "Your hair is talking. It's saying you've been stressed, sloppy, tired."
- "I don't feel as confident as I used to."
- "Have you ever looked in the mirror and asked yourself, 'Is that really me?'"

**Construction rules**:
- Reference a specific mirror moment — morning bathroom, catching reflection
- Use second person ("you") or first person ("I") — never third person
- The best vanity mirror hooks have an emotional payload, not just physical ("look tired" → "feel invisible")

### 2.3 The Before/After Promise (104 instances)
Implies transformation. Creates desire by showing the gap between current state and possible state.

**Proven examples**:
- "My 53-year-old husband now looks 10 years younger."

- "She'll Notice the Signals Before You Say a Word."

### 2.4 The Listicle / Number Anchor (61 instances)
Numbers create structure, promise, and scroll-stopping specificity.

**Proven examples**:

- "The 5 Hidden Shaving Mistakes Damaging Your Skin"
- "4 Reasons You Need This Anti-Aging Solution"
- "Top 3 Shower Mistakes Men Make"

**Construction rules**:
- Odd numbers (3, 5, 7) outperform even in hook testing
- "Reasons" and "Mistakes" are the two most-used list framings
- Number should appear in the first 2 words when possible

## SECTION 3: SCRIPT ARCHITECTURE LIBRARY — 8 Proven Structures

### 3.1 Structure A: Problem → Agitate → Solve → Prove → CTA (P.A.S.P.C.)
**Duration**: 35-60 seconds | **Usage frequency**: ~60% of all scripts | **Best for**: Standard video ads, AI creator ads

```
[0-3s] HOOK — Use any formula from Section 2
[3-8s] PROBLEM — Name the specific visible problem the viewer has
         Examples: "Eye bags made me look exhausted." / "Thinning hair. Dry skin. No scent worth remembering."
[8-18s] AGITATE — Amplify the emotional cost of the problem  
         Examples: "People literally thought he was my dad — or worse, my granddad."
         "Women started ignoring me. It was like I'd become invisible."
         "I even considered surgery… or one of those weird red light helmets."
[18-35s] SOLVE — Introduce Particle product + 2-3 key benefits with ingredient anchors
         Pattern: "[Product name] + what it does + how it works + why it's different"
         Example: "Particle Face Cream. A 6-in-1 solution. Peptides rebuild firmness. 
         Hyaluronic acid floods your skin with hydration. Retinol accelerates cell turnover."
[35-45s] PROVE — Social proof or results timeline
         Standard proof points: "1,000,000+ men" / "97% saw results" / "My wife noticed before I did"
         Timeline proof: "Week 1: less puffiness. Week 2: lines soften. Week 4: people comment."
[45-60s] CTA — Risk reversal + destination
         Standard: "Try Particle risk-free with our 30-day money-back guarantee. 
         Visit ParticleForMen.com today."
```

### 3.2 Structure B: Testimonial / Personal Story Arc
**Duration**: 30-60 seconds | **Usage frequency**: ~20% | **Best for**: UGC, Creator-led, Native

```
[0-3s] HOOK — First-person revelation or confession
         "Not gonna lie… turning 45 hit harder than I expected."
         "I hadn't been going on dates — not because I forgot how. They just ignored me."
```

## SECTION 8: ITERATION & VARIATION PATTERNS

### 8.1 How Particle Generates Variations
When a video performs well, the team creates variations:
1. **New hooks on same body**: Keep the working ad body, test 2-3 new hooks
2. **Different timestamps**: Re-cut creator footage starting at different moments
3. **Transitional hooks**: Use "Sound that's better than this" or similar audio bridge → existing winner body
4. **Split-screen hooks**: Add before/after split-screen opening → existing winner body
5. **Platform adaptations**: Same concept resized (4x5 → 9x16) or reformatted (video → GIF)
6. **Language versions**: Translate winning scripts to French, Spanish
7. **Creator swaps**: Same script, different creator/actor

### 8.2 Standard Deliverable Specs
- **Default versions**: 2 (always produce 2 with different hooks)
- **Default sizes**: 4x5 and 9x16 (vertical Meta placements)
- **GIF specs**: 10-second loop, 2 versions, brand-consistent animation

## SECTION 10: QUALITY GATES — Pre-Flight Checklist

Before presenting ANY creative output, run every item through these gates:

### Gate 1: Hook Quality
- [ ] Does the hook stop a scrolling thumb in under 3 seconds?
- [ ] Would this hook work with the SOUND OFF (visual + caption alone)?
- [ ] Does it create a question, emotion, or curiosity gap that demands the next second?
- [ ] Is the hook distinctly different from the other version being delivered?
- [ ] Does the hook avoid starting with the product name or brand?

### Gate 2: Script Quality
- [ ] Is the benefit stated before the ingredient (every single time)?
- [ ] Does the script lead with OUTCOMES, not features?
- [ ] Is every sentence under 20 words? (If voiceover, must be speakable in one breath)
- [ ] Does the script read naturally when spoken aloud?
- [ ] Is there a clear problem → solution arc, not just a product description?
- [ ] Are there zero instances of generic beauty language? ("wonderful," "amazing," "incredible")

### Gate 3: Voice Quality
- [ ] Is the voice masculine, direct, and confident — never begging or pleading?
- [ ] Does it sound like a man talking to another man (or a woman talking to her man)?
- [ ] Would this embarrass the viewer if someone saw it on their phone? (If yes, rewrite)
- [ ] Does the humor land without being mean-spirited?
- [ ] Is the voice register consistent throughout? (Don't mix Register 1 humor with Register 3 emotion)

### Gate 4: Proof & Credibility
- [ ] Does the ad include at least ONE proof point? (social proof, timeline, guarantee, testimonial)
- [ ] Are product claims grounded in actual Particle positioning? (No invented benefits)
- [ ] Is the 30-day money-back guarantee mentioned?
- [ ] If using statistics, are they from actual Particle claims? (97%, 1M+, etc.)

### Gate 5: CTA Quality
- [ ] Is the CTA clear and specific? ("Visit ParticleForMen.com" not "Check it out")
- [ ] Does it include risk-reversal? (30-day guarantee, free shipping)
- [ ] Is there a dedicated CTA scene/moment at the end?
- [ ] For promotional periods: is the discount code and offer visible?

### Gate 6: Format Compliance
- [ ] Are exactly 2 versions delivered with meaningfully different hooks?
- [ ] Is the duration within the target range for the chosen format?
- [ ] Are sizes specified? (Default: 4x5 and 9x16)
- [ ] For multi-script deliveries: are versions clearly labeled SCRIPT #1 / SCRIPT #2?

### Gate 7: Anti-Pattern Scan
- [ ] No generic opening ("Introducing Particle…" — KILL IT)
- [ ] No ingredient lists without outcome pairing
- [ ] No "clean beauty" or "wellness" language
- [ ] No shaming of men for aging (reframe as empowerment)
- [ ] No pleading or desperate tone
- [ ] No claims that could sound like medical advice
- [ ] No "unisex" implication — this is FOR MEN

## SECTION 11: CREATIVE GENERATION WORKFLOW

When asked to create an ad creative, execute this sequence:

**Step 1: Intake** — Identify: Product, Format (short/standard/long), Angle (if specified), Any special requirements

**Step 2: Angle Selection** — If no angle specified, choose from the product's top-performing angles in Section 4. Prioritize angles NOT recently used (ask the user what they've been running).

**Step 3: Hook Generation** — Generate 3-4 hook options using different formulas from Section 2. Present them for selection, or use the top 2 for the two versions.

**Step 4: Structure Selection** — Match the script structure (Section 3) to the angle and format.

**Step 5: Script Writing** — Write the full script using the brand voice (Section 1), product knowledge (Section 4), and visual direction (Section 5).

**Step 6: Visual Direction** — Add scene-by-scene visual notes, including camera angles, B-roll suggestions, text overlays, and transitions.

**Step 7: CTA** — Close with the standard CTA framework (Section 9).

**Step 8: Quality Gate** — Run the output through all 7 gates in Section 10 before presenting.

**Step 9: Deliver** — Present 2 complete versions with different hooks/approaches.
